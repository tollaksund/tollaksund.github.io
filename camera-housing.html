Problem Statement: Computer Vision at "Chicken Height"

Developing a robust machine learning algorithm for chicken detection poses a unique set of challenges when the camera is positioned at "chicken height." Unlike top-down surveillance, a ground-level perspective introduces extreme occlusion and dynamic lighting variations.

At this height, individual birds frequently block the view of others, making it difficult for standard object detection models to differentiate between overlapping subjects. Furthermore, the high-dust environment and constant movement at eye-level require a housing that preserves the integrity of the training data by keeping the lens pristine and the lighting consistent.

Low-level perspective challenges: Occlusion and variable lighting.

Optical Clarity

Designed to reduce internal reflection and provide essential shade for consistent lighting.

Anti-Peck Geometry

Engineered to reduce perching and protect the structure against aggressive scratching.

Robust Algorithms

The design combats context bias, allowing for the development of a more robust detection algorithm.

Upper Housing: Anti-Perch & Durability

The **Top Part** of the housing is critical for maintaining the position of the camera. To prevent birds from disrupting the field of view, the geometry features steep angles that make perching impossible. This section also serves as a rugged shield, protecting the internal electronics from the constant physical stress of scratching and pecking.

Lower Housing: Lighting & Optical Integrity

The **Bottom Part** is engineered to optimize the quality of the visual data. By using a specialized overhang, the housing creates a shaded environment for the lens, reducing glare and internal reflections that would otherwise confuse a machine learning model.

Machine Learning Impact: By ensuring consistent image quality across different times of day, this design helps eliminate context bias. This prevents the algorithm from learning irrelevant environmental cues, leading to a much more robust poultry detection system.
